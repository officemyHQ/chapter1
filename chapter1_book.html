Before you can win, you need to know the field. This chapter maps the arena — India's commercial real estate market — so every deal, every stakeholder, and every opportunity has a place to land. Every economy runs on capital, land, and people. Commercial real estate is where all three meet. CRE is not a byproduct of economic growth; it is the infrastructure that makes growth possible.

CRE does not follow cities; it builds them. Gurgaon, Pune, Hyderabad, Bengaluru were not planned by policy. They were predicted by leases. Over 70% of new-age job creation since 2015 sits within 5 km of a new office district. Real estate does not chase demand. It manufactures it.

The multiplier is real: every rupee invested in CRE generates three to four rupees in downstream activity across transport, retail, and services. Behind one new office are 200 vendors and 20 industries. As the sector digitalises, with proptech funding hitting ₹1.08 lakh crore globally in 2024, those at the intersection of real estate, data, and advisory are best placed to define what comes next.

01

Indian Real Estate: The Big Picture

India's real estate sector is the third largest in the world by value, after only the US and China. Every developer, operator, advisor, and platform here is playing in a global arena. The sector contributes 7% of India's GDP today, roughly $210 billion, with that share projected to reach 13% by 2030.

It is also one of India's biggest employers. Real estate and construction together support over 70 million jobs, second only to agriculture. 1 in 10 non-farm workers in India earns a living from this sector. By 2030, the market is on track to cross $1 trillion in value, driven by urbanisation, business formalisation, and India's rise as a global hub. These are structural shifts. They do not reverse.

Within this sector, real estate splits into two distinct worlds: residential and commercial. Residential is where people live. Commercial is where business happens. The two operate on different logic, different cycles, and different stakeholders.

02

Residential vs Commercial

Real estate is one term, but it represents two fundamentally different markets.

Residential real estate is deeply personal. People buy homes to build a life, provide security for their families, improve their lifestyle, and create a place they can call their own. For most families, purchasing a home is one of the largest emotional and financial decisions they will ever make. It is driven as much by aspiration and sentiment as it is by economics.

Commercial real estate, by contrast, is driven by business needs. Companies require land and infrastructure to build products, deliver services, hire talent, serve customers, and generate economic activity. Offices, retail stores, warehouses, factories, logistics parks, hotels, data centers, and industrial facilities are all forms of commercial real estate that enable businesses to operate and grow.

This distinction matters because the two markets operate differently. A family may spend years planning the purchase of a home and make that decision only once or twice in a lifetime. Businesses, however, evaluate real estate through a structured lens — assessing location, cost, customer access, talent availability, operational efficiency, and scalability.

A defining characteristic of commercial real estate is that it serves recurring business needs. As companies grow, hire employees, launch new products, enter new markets, optimize operations, or adapt to changing market conditions, their space requirements evolve. This creates ongoing demand for offices, retail stores, warehouses, factories, logistics facilities, and other commercial assets throughout the lifecycle of a business.

What begins as a registered office, small retail outlet, or workshop can expand into corporate headquarters, branch offices, manufacturing facilities, distribution centers, industrial parks, and large-scale campuses. Businesses regularly expand, relocate, consolidate, renew leases, and reconfigure spaces to align with changing strategies and operational requirements. Commercial real estate therefore becomes a strategic business function rather than a one-time transaction.

That is what makes commercial real estate fundamentally different. It is not simply about occupying space; it is about enabling commerce. Every office, store, warehouse, factory, hotel, data center, and industrial facility exists because a business needs a place to build, sell, serve, distribute, innovate, or grow.

Residential real estate is about where people live. Commercial real estate is about where economic activity happens. One fulfills personal aspirations; the other powers business growth, employment, investment, and economic development.

03

Commercial Real Estate (CRE)

Commercial Real Estate (CRE) refers to properties used for business, commercial, industrial, institutional, or income-generating activities. It forms the physical infrastructure that enables economic activity and business growth. Every organisation — from a startup to a multinational corporation — requires space at different stages of its journey to operate, serve customers, manufacture products, store inventory, collaborate with employees, and expand into new markets.

Commercial real estate is the physical infrastructure that makes economic activity possible — the offices where deals get closed, the warehouses where goods get dispatched, the campuses where 10,000 employees show up on Monday morning. It's not glamorous when you describe it that way. But zoom out, and it's one of the largest asset classes on the planet.

CRE covers a lot of ground. Grade A office towers in BKC. Last-mile logistics parks outside Pune. Retail high streets in Connaught Place. Industrial corridors along the Delhi-Mumbai Expressway. Each segment operates under different economics, different demand drivers, and different buyer profiles — but they share one thing: every single one requires a business on the other side of the transaction.

CRE is not a monolith. It's a system — of developers who build, operators who run, occupiers who lease, and investors who hold. Each player has a different definition of value, a different time horizon, and a different way of making decisions. The deals that happen inside this system range from a 10-seat coworking booking to a 500,000 sq ft campus pre-commitment. Same industry. Very different conversations.

04

Office Leasing: Conventional vs Flex

Within office real estate, companies today have two primary ways to occupy space: conventional leasing and flex leasing.

Conventional leasing is the traditional model. A company leases space directly from a developer or landlord, signs a long-term lease, mostly invests in fit-outs and furniture, manages facility operations, and takes responsibility for running the office. This approach offers maximum customization and control but requires significant capital investment, longer setup timelines, and multi-year commitments.

Flex leasing follows a different model. Instead of dealing directly with the building owner, the company leases space from a flexible workspace operator that has already designed, furnished, and operationalized the office. The operator manages infrastructure, amenities, maintenance, and day-to-day operations, allowing the occupier to move in and become operational almost immediately. Contracts are typically more flexible, with lower upfront investment and the ability to scale space up or down as business needs change.

Where myHQ Fits

Every office leasing transaction typically involves two important intermediaries: a platform that enables digital search and discovery, and a consultant who guides the client through the transaction process.

Consultants (IPCs and DPCs) bring market knowledge, relationships, transaction expertise, and negotiation capability. They help occupiers evaluate options, benchmark commercial terms, and navigate complex lease negotiations. However, their discovery process is primarily offline and relationship-driven, making it difficult to provide the transparency, speed, and real-time visibility that modern occupiers increasingly expect from a digital search experience.

Listing Platforms solve the digital search and discovery problem by aggregating available options in one place. They provide transparency, scale, and self-service access to the market. However, they typically offer limited advisory support. Clients can browse hundreds of spaces but may still struggle to determine which options best align with their business requirements, budget, growth plans, workplace strategy, and commercial objectives.

myHQ combines both models. It brings together the scale and transparency of a digital marketplace with the expertise of a leasing consultant. Clients benefit from broad market visibility while also receiving guidance on requirement assessment, space shortlisting, site evaluations, commercial negotiations, and deal closure.

Rather than acting solely as a listing platform or solely as a consultant, myHQ integrates discovery, advisory, and transaction execution into a single experience — helping occupiers find the right workspace faster and transact with greater confidence.
